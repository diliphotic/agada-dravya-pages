धत्तूर (DHATTŪRA)
Upaviṣa | Cerebral Poison | Sthāvara Viṣa
Latin Name: Dhatura metel Linn.
Family: Solanaceae
English Name: Thorn Apple
Type of Poison: Cerebral Poison
Habitat: Fields, pastures, roadsides
Poisonous Parts: Leaves, seeds, flowers, roots

Introduction

Dhattūra is a tall, erect annual or perennial herb belonging to the family Solanaceae. It grows commonly along roadsides, wastelands, and open fields throughout India.

The plant is known for its large trumpet-shaped flowers and spiny fruits.

In Ayurveda, Dhattūra is described as an Upaviṣa and is used only in very small, purified doses.

All parts of the plant contain toxic alkaloids such as atropine, hyoscine, and hyoscyamine which act on the nervous system.

Despite its toxicity, Dhattūra has medicinal uses as an antispasmodic, analgesic, bronchodilator, and sedative when properly processed.

Sources of Exposure

Accidental:
Seeds or leaves mistaken for edible plants (kalonji/mustard)
Improper herbal decoctions or home remedies
Children ingesting seeds or flowers

Criminal:
Food adulteration for robbery and stupefying victims
Added to alcohol or prasad
Used as Aupadravika Viṣa (stupefying poison)

Morphology

Habit: Erect herb/shrub, 1–2 m tall, strong unpleasant odour, highly toxic
Root: Tap root system
Stem: Erect, stout, cylindrical, green to purplish, much branched
Leaves: Alternate, simple, ovate to broadly ovate, large (10–20 cm), reticulate venation
Inflorescence: Solitary, axillary or terminal
Flowers: Large, showy, fragrant; white/violet/purple; actinomorphic; bisexual
Fruit: Capsule covered with sharp spines
Seeds: Numerous, kidney-shaped, brown to black, rough, highly poisonous

Toxicological Profile

Fatal Dose: 50–100 seeds / 10–100 mg atropine
Fatal Period: 24 hours

Active Principles:
Tropane alkaloids, Dhaturin, Hyoscyamine, Atropine, Fixed oil

Signs & Symptoms (Modern)

Dysphagia (difficulty in swallowing)
Drowsiness
Dilated pupils
Delirium with confusion and disorientation
Dysuria
Diplopia
Dry hot skin
Drunken gait (ataxia)
Death due to respiratory failure

मुखशोष – Dry mouth
जिह्वापरुषता – Dry tongue
तृष्णा – Thirst
मूर्च्छा – Fainting
उन्माद – Insanity
प्रलाप – Excessive speech
मोह – Confusion
ज्वर – Fever

Detoxification (Śodhana)

Swedana Śodhana:
Seeds cleaned, tied in pottali, subjected to Dolāyantra Swedana using kanjika or gomūtra for 3–6 hours, washed and shade dried.

Gomūtra Śodhana:
Seeds subjected to swedana in gomūtra using Dolāyantra to reduce viṣatva and tīkṣṇatā.

Differential Diagnosis

Belladonna
Cannabis intoxication
Alcohol or drug intoxication
Mushroom poisoning

Postmortem Appearance

Datura seeds in stomach
Signs of asphyxia
Contracted internal organs
Seeds persist after decomposition
Nasal septum ulceration/perforation
Injection scars in chronic abusers
Hepatic necrosis in paste smokers
Myocardial infarction
Pulmonary edema
GI tract inflammation

Formulations

Kanakāsava: Bronchodilator, antispasmodic, antitussive; relief in Śvāsa & Kāsa; Vāta-Kapha śāmaka

Mahākanakā Taila: Analgesic, Vāta-śāmaka, neurological relief, improves circulation

Management

Modern:
Initial assessment, decontamination, pilocarpine as antidote, symptomatic treatment (Morphine avoided)

Ayurvedic:
Viṣa cikitsā, śamana cikitsā, upaśamana

Treatment

Modern: Emesis, gastric lavage, pilocarpine

Ayurvedic:
Honey with cow’s milk
Vrintaka fruit juice as antidote

Medicolegal Aspects

Known as roadside poison
Used in robbery, theft, kidnapping
Occasionally used for suicide or homicide
Sudden poisoning common
