औद्दालक (AUDDĀLAKA)
Cardiac Poison | Sthāvara Viṣa
Latin Name: Cerbera odollam
Family: Apocynaceae
Sanskrit Name: औद्दालक
Vernacular Names: Dabur, Sukan, Suicide Tree, Pong-pong Tree, Othalum Tree
Classification: Cardiac Poison
Poisonous Part: Seed (Kernel)

Introduction

Auddālaka (Cerbera odollam) is a small evergreen tree found along coastal regions of tropical countries. It is native to India and other parts of Southern Asia and grows in coastal salt swamps, marshy areas, riverbanks, and mangrove margins.

The tree resembles oleander, and its fruits resemble unripe mangoes. It is notorious for its lethal cardiac glycosides and is commonly known as the “Suicide Tree.”

Primary Sources of Exposure

Deliberate Self-Harm (Suicide):
Most common source, especially in South India (Kerala & Tamil Nadu). Seeds are easily accessible and ingestion is a frequent method of suicide, particularly among women.

Homicide:
Cerberin is difficult to detect in routine autopsies, and its bitter taste can be masked by spicy food. Seeds are available online, increasing misuse potential.

Accidental Ingestion:
Occurs due to proximity of the tree in gardens and coastal areas, especially in children.

Secondary Poisoning:
Reported in South Pacific islands after consumption of coconut crabs that fed on Cerbera fruits.

Contemporary Scientific Importance

Global availability through online seed trade.
Poor awareness among Western physicians and forensic experts.
Diagnostic difficulty as cerberin is not detected in routine toxicology screens.
Detection requires LC–MS/MS techniques.
Used in biopesticides, insect repellents, and biodiesel research.

Morphology

Tree: Medium evergreen tree, 15–20 m tall, bushy crown
Trunk: Rough, grey bark; exudes white latex when cut
Habitat: Coastal swamps, mangroves, riverbanks

Flowers:
Terminal cymes; trumpet-shaped white flowers with yellow eye; sweet jasmine-like fragrance

Fruit:
Oval drupe; green → reddish-purple → dark brown/black on ripening
Thick woody husk enclosing a single seed

Seed:
Large, fleshy kernel turning violet/grey/black on exposure; highly poisonous
Buoyant fruit aids water dispersal

Active Principles

Cardiac glycosides – Cerberin

Mode of Action

Cerberin inhibits Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase in cardiac muscle, leading to severe bradyarrhythmia, conduction defects, hyperkalemia, and fatal cardiac failure.

Fatal Dose & Fatal Period

Fatal Dose: Kernel of one fruit
Fatal Period: 1–2 days

Post-Mortem Findings

Congested eyes
Congestion of internal organs
Lungs congested and edematous
Subepicardial, subendocardial & subpleural petechial hemorrhages
Congested gastric mucosa with hemorrhages and gastritis
Findings resemble asphyxia

Treatment

Gastric lavage
Atropine 0.5 mg IV for bradyarrhythmia
Correction of hyperkalemia
Cardiac pacing
Symptomatic and supportive treatment

Medicolegal Aspects

Common suicidal poison in Tamil Nadu & Kerala
Accidental poisoning in children
Powdered kernel mixed with alcohol for homicide
Difficult detection of cerberin in autopsy

Mode of Use in Viṣa Cases

Leaf extracts studied for cytotoxic activity against breast (T47D, MCF-7) and ovarian cancer cell lines showing significant anticancer activity.

Detoxification (Śodhana) Method

Seeds broken and kernel removed.
Kernels soaked in cow’s urine (Go-mutra) for 3–7 days with daily replacement.
Followed by boiling in cow’s milk (Go-dugdha) for 3 hours in Dolāyantra.
