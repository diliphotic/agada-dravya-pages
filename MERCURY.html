पारद (MERCURY)
स्थावर विष | Poison of Metallic Origin
Name: Hydrargyrum
English Name: Mercury (Quick Silver)
Sanskrit Name: पारद
Hindi Name: पारा
Chemical Formula: Hg
Atomic Number: 80
Atomic Weight: 200.61
Relative Atomic Mass: 200.50 g/mole
Atomic Volume: 14.8 cc
Ionic Radius (+2): 1.10

Introduction

Mercury is a silvery white metal, liquid at room temperature with high density (13.6). It is divisible into spherical globules, mobile, without any odour or taste, cold to touch, and slowly volatilizes at ordinary temperature.

Mercury has no poisonous effect when swallowed, but mercury vapours are highly poisonous when inhaled. Low melting and boiling points are due to its large atomic size.

The metallic shine of mercury is due to the presence of free electrons with high plasma frequency. It is soluble in nitric acid and in boiling sulphuric acid. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

Fatal Dose & Fatal Period

Fatal Dose:
Mercuric Chloride – 1.5 g
Methyl Mercury – 10.60 mg
Mercury Vapour – 10 mg/m³

Fatal Period: 3–5 days

Sources of Exposure (With Contemporary Relevance)

Natural Sources:
Volcanic eruptions, weathering of mercury-containing rocks, forest fires, natural evaporation from soil and water bodies

Environmental Sources:
Air pollution from coal-fired power plants, industrial waste in rivers and lakes, contaminated soil and water, broken fluorescent lamps and thermometers

Occupational Sources:
Gold mining and refining, thermometer and barometer manufacturing, battery manufacturing, chemical and pharmaceutical industries, paint and pigment industries

Medicinal & Cosmetic Sources:
Improper use of Ayurvedic Rasashastra preparations, unregulated traditional medicines, skin-lightening creams, old antiseptics and ointments

Dietary Sources:
Fish and seafood (tuna, shark, swordfish), contaminated grains and vegetables, food grown in mercury-polluted soil (methyl mercury bioaccumulation)

Household Sources:
Broken thermometers, fluorescent tube lights, old electrical switches, disinfectants, fungicides

Accidental & Intentional:
Accidental ingestion by children, inhalation of vapours, intentional poisoning (rare)

Signs & Symptoms

Metallic taste, burning in mouth and throat
Severe abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting with blood
Hemorrhagic diarrhea
Shock due to fluid loss
Acute renal failure (anuria, acute tubular necrosis)
Mercurial tremors (Danbury tremors, Hatter’s shake)
Concussion mercurialis
Mercurial erethism (Mad Hatter disease)
Memory loss, insomnia, irritability, depression
Acrodynia (Pink disease) in children
Mercuria lentis (brownish deposit on lens)
Membranous glomerulonephritis
Burtonian line on gums

Differential Diagnosis

Parkinsonism, Alzheimer’s disease, stroke, multiple sclerosis
Lead, arsenic, cadmium, thallium poisoning
Meningitis, encephalitis
Acid/alkali ingestion, GI bleed
Thiamine deficiency, metabolic encephalopathy
Autoimmune disorders, nephrotic syndrome

Management

Remove exposure immediately
Supportive care – hydration, organ support, GI decontamination
Chelation: BAL, DMSA, DMPS (as indicated)
Severe cases – haemodialysis or plasma exchange
Chronic poisoning – chelation optional, damage mostly irreversible

Treatment

Remove patient from exposure
Maintain oral hygiene
Add milk to diet
Administer purgatives and demulcents
BAL intramuscular injection
Protein-rich diet
Symptomatic management

Postmortem Appearance

Cyanosis of face and extremities
Grayish-white corrosion around mouth (acute ingestion)
GI tract with ashen-gray mucosa, pseudo-membranes, petechial hemorrhages
Necrotic colitis of large intestine
Kidneys enlarged – “Large white kidney”, acute tubular necrosis
Lungs – acute pneumonitis, pulmonary edema
Chronic cases – Burtonian line, cerebellar atrophy, cortical degeneration

Mode of Use in Viṣa Cases

Used only after Śodhana
Mostly administered as Kajjalī (Parada + Gandhaka)
Included in Rasa Sindūra, Mṛtyuñjaya Rasa, Sūtaśekhara
Given in micro-dose with ghṛta or madhu
Raw mercury strictly contraindicated

Formulations & Uses

Parada Bhasma: Chronic poisoning, skin disorders
Kajjalī: Chronic toxicity, viṣaghna action
Rasa Sindūra: Dūṣī viṣa, rasāyana
Makardhwaja: Viṣajanya daurbalya, immunity
Rasa Māṇikya: Toxic skin disorders, chronic ulcers

Tests to Detect Mercury

Blood mercury – acute exposure
24-hour urine mercury – chronic exposure
Hair analysis – long-term exposure
Kidney function tests – proteinuria
Liver enzymes, nerve conduction studies

Ayurvedic: Rakta, Mutra, Mala parīkṣā; Jihvā, Netra, Nāḍī parīkṣā

Medicolegal Aspects

Not commonly used for suicide or homicide
Accidental poisoning common in children due to shiny appearance
Toxicity from mercury-containing medicines and cosmetics
