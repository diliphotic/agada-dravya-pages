गुञ्जा (GUÑJĀ)
Upaviṣa | Vanaspati Viṣa | Highly Toxic Plant Poison
Latin Name: Abrus precatorius
English Name: Rosary pea, Indian licorice
Family: Fabaceae (Leguminosae)
Ayurvedic Classification: उपविष
Modern Classification: Plant toxin (Toxalbumin)
Poisonous Part: Seeds

Introduction

Guñjā (Abrus precatorius) is a highly poisonous woody climber described extensively in Ayurvedic as well as modern toxicology literature.

In its raw form, it is extremely toxic due to the presence of abrin. After proper Śodhana, it is used therapeutically in Ayurveda.

Contemporary Science – Sources & Routes of Exposure

1. Injection (Most dangerous):
Paste of Gunja seed introduced using a needle-like instrument (sui). Abrin enters bloodstream causing rapid inhibition of protein synthesis, local necrosis, and systemic toxicity. Seen commonly in homicidal poisoning.

2. Ingestion:
Whole seeds may pass harmlessly, but chewed or crushed seeds release abrin leading to severe GI irritation, vomiting, diarrhea, and organ failure.

3. Topical / Percutaneous Absorption:
Improper lepa or taila application on broken skin may cause local necrosis and systemic absorption.

4. Occupational / Environmental Exposure:
Handling seeds used in jewelry, rosaries, toys, or traditional medicine preparation. Abrin dust may cause skin or mucosal toxicity.

5. Bioterrorism Potential:
Abrin is classified as a Category B biotoxin. Routes include injection, ingestion, and experimental inhalation. No specific antidote is available.

Traditional / Ayurvedic Perspective

Guñjā is classified as Upaviṣa and requires mandatory Śodhana. After purification, it is used as Vajīkaraṇa, Keśya, and Kuṣṭhaghna drug.

Modern Medical Perspective

Abrin is a highly potent toxalbumin causing irreversible inhibition of protein synthesis. There is no specific antidote. Management is purely supportive.

Fatal Dose & Fatal Period

Fatal Dose: 1–2 chewed seeds
Fatal Period: 1–3 days

Detoxification (Śodhana)

Seeds boiled in cow’s milk or castor oil for 3 hours

OR

Seeds soaked in lemon juice for 3 days with daily replacement, followed by removal of outer coat and shade drying.

Conclusion

Guñjā is a highly potent plant poison with significant therapeutic value after proper purification. Thorough understanding is essential for Agada Tantra practice.
