करवीर (KARAVĪRA)
Upaviṣa | Cardiac Poison | Sthāvara Viṣa
Botanical Name: Nerium indicum
Family: Apocynaceae
Sanskrit Synonyms: करवीर, हयारि, हयमार, अश्वमार
Poisonous Part: All parts (Roots most toxic)
Type of Poison: Cardiac glycoside poison

Introduction

Karavīra is a well-known poisonous plant described in Ayurveda belonging to the family Apocynaceae. It is commonly found throughout India and is mainly used externally in medicine.

Internal use is generally avoided due to its strong toxicity. All parts of the plant contain cardiac glycosides which predominantly affect the heart and nervous system.

Sources of Exposure

Ornamental Exposure: Grown in gardens, road dividers, schools and hospitals; accidental ingestion by children.

Medicinal / Herbal Misuse: Improper identification or lack of Śodhana in folk medicine and self-medication.

Suicidal Poisoning: Oral ingestion of paste or decoction of leaves, flowers or roots.

Smoke Inhalation: Burning of wood or dry branches used as fuel or rituals.

Food Contamination: Cooking using Karavīra sticks or firewood causing leaching of toxins.

Veterinary Exposure: Cattle poisoning by ingestion of leaves or root juice.

Water Contamination: Leaves or flowers falling into wells or open water sources.

Mode of Action (Viṣa Kriyā)

↓

↓

Severe cardiotoxicity

↓

Circulatory failure and death

Toxic Part, Fatal Dose & Fatal Period

Toxicity Order: Seeds = Roots > Fruits > Leaves

Fatal Dose:
Leaves: 15–20 in number
Roots: 15–20 g

Fatal Period: 24 hours

Morphology

Habit: Evergreen shrub or small tree (2–6 m)
Root: Tap root system
Stem: Cylindrical, woody, branched, milky latex present
Leaves: Opposite or whorled, lanceolate, simple
Flowers: Large, showy, fragrant
Inflorescence: Terminal cymose clusters
Fruit: Dehiscent
Seeds: Numerous, brown, oblong

Signs & Symptoms

Gastrointestinal: Nausea, severe vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhoea

Cardiovascular: Bradycardia or tachycardia, irregular pulse, palpitations, hypotension, arrhythmias, cardiac arrest

Nervous System: Dizziness, headache, confusion, restlessness, weakness, seizures (severe cases)

Visual: Blurred vision, yellow/green vision (xanthopsia), halos

Electrolyte Imbalance: Hyperkalemia, muscle weakness

Respiratory: Dyspnea, respiratory distress (late stage)

Medicolegal Importance

Accidental poisoning in children and animals
Smoke inhalation poisoning
Suicidal poisoning in rural areas
Cattle poisoning using root juice or crushed seeds

Management (Ayurveda)

Vamana – Madanaphala, Yaṣṭimadhu
Virechana – Trivṛt, Eraṇḍa taila
Dhūmapāna / Nasya
Daha-prashamana – Milk, ghee, sugar water, coconut water
Viṣaghna dravya – Guḍūcī, Musta, Haridrā, Sārivā, Nimba
Hṛdaya rakṣaṇa – Arjuna kaṣāya, ghṛta, dugdha
Jīvanīya dravya – Drākṣā, Madhuka

Management (Modern)

Gastric lavage, activated charcoal
Continuous ECG monitoring
IV fluids and oxygen therapy
Digoxin-specific Fab fragments (Digibind / DigiFab)
Atropine for bradycardia
Lidocaine / Phenytoin for arrhythmias
Correct electrolytes cautiously (avoid calcium)

Postmortem Appearance

Congestion of organs
Petechial hemorrhages on heart surface
Root fragments in stomach
Resistant to heat – detectable even in burnt bodies

Śodhana

Boiled in Dolāyantra filled with Go-dugdha for one prahara.

Formulations & Uses

Karavīra Mūla Kalka: External use in viṣa-daṁśa – Viṣaghna, Śothahara

Karavīra Patra Svarasa: External lepa in kuṣṭha, kaṇḍu, krimi-janya vikāra

Karavīra Taila: External use in viṣa-vikāra, śotha, vraṇa

Karavīra Kṣāra: External use in krimi and duṣṭa vraṇa

Karavīra Dhūpa: Dhūpana for krimi and viṣa-jantu nāśana

Research Work

Leaf extract shows anticancer activity
Yellow Karavīra flowers used as weight-loss supplement, antiseptic, antibacterial, antifungal and anti-inflammatory agents
