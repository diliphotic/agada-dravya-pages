ताम्र (TĀMRA / COPPER)
स्थावर विष – धातुमूल विष (Copper)
Chemical Name: Copper Sulphate
Chemical Formula: CuSO₄
Atomic Number: 29
Nature: Metallic poison (salts)
Poisonous Form: Copper salts
Modern Classification: Metallic Irritant Poison

Introduction

Copper is an essential trace element required for normal physiological functions. Metallic copper in pure form is non-toxic; however, copper salts—especially copper sulphate—are highly poisonous.

Copper sulphate is a blue crystalline, odorless compound with a metallic taste and is freely soluble in water.

Classification

Ayurveda: स्थावर विष, धातुमूल विष

Modern: Metallic irritant poison

Source of Exposure (Viṣa Saṃsparśa Mārgāḥ)

Drinking water from copper pipes, nuts and seeds, legumes, green leafy vegetables, whole grains.
Air, soil, and water contamination near mines and industrial areas.
Ayurvedic preparations containing ताम्र भस्म.
Pesticide and fungicide manufacturing, welding, metal polishing.

Toxicological Profile

Fatal Dose: 10–20 g of copper sulphate

Fatal Period: 12–24 hours

Mechanism: Local corrosive and systemic toxicity

Mode of Action (Viṣa Kriyā)

Copper salts ingestion → Coagulation necrosis of mucosa

↓

Absorption into circulation

↓

Hemolysis → Hepatic damage → Renal damage

↓

Metabolic acidosis, shock, multi-organ failure

Signs & Symptoms (Lakṣaṇa)

वान्ति (Vomiting)
भ्रान्ति (Giddiness)
मुख शोष (Dryness of mouth)
दाह (Burning sensation)
Metallic taste, burning pain in mouth, throat, and abdomen
Blue or green colored vomiting
Diarrhoea, sometimes blood-mixed
Excessive thirst, dehydration, myalgia
Collapse and death in severe cases
Fatigue, headache, anorexia
Green discoloration of hair
Kayser–Fleischer rings in cornea
Wilson’s disease (genetic copper metabolism disorder)

Diagnosis & Management

Diagnosis: Blood copper level > 1.5 mg/100 ml

Management:

  • Demulcents: Milk or egg white
  • Chelation therapy: D-Penicillamine or BAL
  • Haemodialysis in severe cases
  • Supportive care: IV fluids, bicarbonate, oxygen

Treatment (चिकित्सा)

Haemodialysis in early stages of poisoning
Gastric lavage with potassium ferrocyanide

Antidote

D-Penicillamine – primary chelating agent for copper toxicity

Postmortem Appearance

Jaundiced skin and conjunctiva
Corrosive changes in mouth and oesophagus
Hepatic and renal necrosis

Tests to Detect Poison

Serum copper levels
Liver function tests

Medicolegal Aspects

Commonly suicidal due to easy availability
Accidental ingestion through contaminated food or water
Homicidal use is rare
Occupational exposure in metal industries
