नाग (LEAD)
Sthāvara Viṣa | Dhātu-mūla Viṣa | Metallic Poison
Ayurvedic Name: नाग
English Name: Lead
Nature: Heavy metal
Category: Metallic poison
State: Soft, heavy, malleable metal

Introduction

A lead drug is an early chemical compound that shows promising biological activity and serves as the starting point for developing a new medicine. It is further optimized to improve its effectiveness and safety.

Properties

Lead is a soft, heavy and malleable element.
Pure lead appears white like silver but tarnishes to a dull gray color when exposed to air.
It is not found in its pure form in nature and is commonly mixed with other metals.
Lead does not dissolve in water at normal temperatures but reacts with oxygen-containing water to form hydroxides.

Sources

Lead is obtained as a mineral in ores along with other metals such as copper, zinc, silver and gold.

Primary minerals:
Galena (PbS)
Cerussite (Lead carbonate)
Anglesite (Lead sulfate)
Crocoisite (Lead chromate)
Massicot (Lead oxide)
Coturnirite (Lead chloride)

Toxic Compounds

Lead acetate (Sugar of lead)
Lead chromate (Litharge)
Lead carbonate (White lead)
TEL (Tetra ethyl lead)

Action

Lead adversely affects mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation.
Lead causes hemolysis of mature RBCs.
Accumulation in the brain damages nerve cells and their myelin sheath, causing cerebral edema.

Fatal Dose & Fatal Period

Lead carbonate – 40 g
Lead acetate – 20 g

Fatal Period: 2–3 days

Signs & Symptoms of Acute Poisoning

Metallic taste
Headache
Intense thirst
Bloody diarrhea

Diagnosis

Clinical features
Complete blood count & peripheral smear
Blood lead level assessment

Treatment

Removal from the source of poisoning
Chelation therapy
BAL – 4 mg/kg immediately
Calcium gluconate for abdominal colic

Post-Mortem Findings

Fat depletion in muscles
Bluish line on gums
Erosion and constriction of gastric mucosa
Hardening and constriction of liver and kidneys
Inflammation and yellow discoloration of brain
Enlargement of heart

Medicolegal Aspects

Severe lead poisoning and suicide are rare
Mostly accidental or chronic poisoning
Not used for homicide
Associated with animal slaughter and criminal abortion
Causes permanent brain damage and death

Sources of Exposure of Viṣa

Occupational: Battery manufacturing and recycling
Environmental: Drinking water from areas of lead mining
Domestic & Consumer: Lead-based paints
Food related: Cosmetics or traditional medicines

Antidotes

Systemic antidote / neutralizer not documented

Modern:
Intralipid 20% (Lipid Rescue Therapy)
Supportive: Diazepam for seizures

Important Formulations

Lidocaine Injection (1–2%) – infiltration, nerve blocks
Lidocaine + Adrenaline Injection – prolonged anesthesia
Lidocaine Gel / Ointment (2–5%) – mucosal anesthesia
